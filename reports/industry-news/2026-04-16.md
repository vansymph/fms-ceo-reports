## 2. Meta Faces Major Backlash Over Facial Recognition in Ray-Ban Smart Glasses
**Category:** AI / Face Recognition / Regulatory Risk 🔍

70+ advocacy groups (including ACLU) are demanding Meta abandon its "Name Tag" feature — facial recognition built into Ray-Ban smart glasses that could identify strangers in public from social media photos. Two versions reportedly in development: one that recognizes your social contacts, one that identifies *anyone* with a public profile. The coalition is calling it "a red line society must not cross." For FMS: this is a double-edged signal. The backlash confirms public wariness around face recognition at scale — meaning FMS must maintain its consent-based framing (athletes opt into bib search, their photos are already public at race events). But the regulatory heat could ultimately shape rules that affect any face-recognition product in consumer or semi-public contexts. Keep the FMS narrative clean: bib search, not surveillance.

## 3. Canon's SPAD Sensor Camera (MS-510) Sees in Near-Total Darkness — Unveiled at NAB 2026
**Category:** Camera Technology / Event Photography 📷

Canon unveiled the MS-510 at NAB 2026 — an industrial camera using a Single Photon Avalanche Diode (SPAD) image sensor that captures individual photons rather than accumulated light. The result: usable images in near-zero-light conditions, extremely low noise at high ISOs, and near-zero motion blur even in darkness. This isn't a consumer camera, but the underlying tech is significant. Race events increasingly run at night or in early morning darkness (think HOKA Midnight Run, BGC night races). As SPAD tech trickles into professional bodies over the next 2-3 years, FMS photographers gain a serious capability upgrade for challenging race conditions — meaning more usable shots per event, higher conversion potential.
